The Hidden Costs of Going Back to the Office (And How to Calculate Them)

Most employees think about their salary when evaluating an RTO mandate. Few think about the $12,000 or more per year they quietly spend just to show up. Here's a detailed breakdown of every hidden cost of returning to the office -- and the free tools to calculate your real number.

The number that should make you pause: According to a 2025 Owl Labs survey, the average fully in-office worker spends $51 per day on commuting, meals, and incidental costs -- adding up to over $12,000 per year for a typical 5-day schedule. For hybrid workers going in 3 days per week, that still totals roughly $8,000 annually. Yet fewer than 1 in 5 workers have ever calculated their actual cost of going to the office.

The True Cost of Returning to the Office

When your company announces a return-to-office policy, the conversation almost always centers on flexibility, culture, and collaboration. What rarely gets discussed is the financial burden that RTO policies shift squarely onto employees. Your employer doesn't pay for your gas. They don't reimburse your parking. They don't cover the lunch you used to make at home for free. And they certainly don't compensate you for the hours you lose sitting in traffic.

The result is what economists call a "shadow pay cut" -- a reduction in your effective compensation that never shows up on a pay stub. When you go from working remotely to commuting five days a week, your salary stays the same, but your take-home financial position can deteriorate by thousands of dollars per year.

A 2025 study from the National Bureau of Economic Research found that the average American worker values the ability to work from home 2-3 days per week at roughly 8% of their salary. That's not an abstract preference -- it reflects the real financial and time costs that remote work eliminates. When a full RTO mandate strips that away, employees are effectively accepting an 8% pay cut without any change to their nominal compensation.

The hidden costs of going back to the office fall into five major categories: commuting, wardrobe and grooming, food and meals, childcare and pet care, and lost time. Let's break down each one with real numbers.

Commuting Costs Breakdown

Commuting is the largest and most obvious cost of going to the office -- yet most workers dramatically underestimate what they actually spend. That's because commute costs are fragmented across gas stations, transit cards, parking meters, and vehicle maintenance, making the total easy to ignore.

Driving costs

The IRS standard mileage rate for 2026 is $0.70 per mile. This rate accounts for gas, insurance, depreciation, maintenance, and repairs -- all the costs of operating a vehicle. For an employee with a 25-mile one-way commute (the national average), the daily round-trip driving cost comes to:

50 miles x $0.70 = $35.00 per day

Over a 5-day work week, that's $175. Over 50 working weeks (accounting for holidays and PTO), that's $8,750 per year -- just for driving.

But that's just the average. Workers in sprawling metro areas like Houston, Atlanta, or the Inland Empire often face 35-45 mile one-way commutes, pushing annual driving costs above $12,000. And these figures don't include the toll roads that many commuters rely on to save time, which can add another $5-$15 per day.

Public transit costs

Public transit is often cheaper than driving, but it's far from free. A monthly unlimited MetroCard in New York City costs $132 ($1,584 per year). A monthly BART pass in the San Francisco Bay Area averages $150-$200 depending on distance. In Chicago, a monthly CTA pass runs $75, but suburban Metra commuters pay $170-$240 per month.

Nationally, the average public transit commuter spends approximately $100-$150 per month, or $1,200-$1,800 per year. And unlike driving, transit commuters often need to add the cost of getting to and from the station -- whether that's a short Uber ride, a parking fee at a park-and-ride lot, or a second bus fare.

Parking costs

For drivers, parking is a separate and often staggering expense. The average monthly parking rate in a U.S. downtown area is $250, according to Parkopedia's 2025 report. In the most expensive cities, rates are far higher:

Manhattan: $500-$600/month. San Francisco: $350-$450/month. Boston: $350-$400/month. Chicago: $250-$350/month. Seattle: $250-$300/month.

Even in mid-tier cities, monthly parking runs $100-$200. Over a year, that's $1,200-$7,200 in parking alone -- a cost that simply doesn't exist when you work from home.

Wardrobe and Grooming Costs

One of the most frequently overlooked costs of going to the office is what you wear to get there. Remote workers famously live in athleisure, and while that's become something of a joke, the financial reality behind it is serious. Professional clothing is expensive, it wears out faster when worn regularly, and the grooming standards of an office environment add costs that simply vanish when you work from home.

Professional clothing

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the average American household spends $1,945 per year on apparel and services. For workers in office environments with business casual or business professional dress codes, personal spending on work-appropriate clothing is significantly higher. Industry surveys suggest that office workers spend $1,000-$2,500 per year specifically on work clothes -- separate from casual and weekend clothing.

This includes not just the clothes themselves but also dry cleaning (averaging $15-$30 per week for workers in professional environments), shoe maintenance, and the faster replacement cycle that comes with daily office wear. A pair of dress shoes worn 5 days a week needs replacement far sooner than one worn occasionally.

Grooming and personal care

Office attendance drives grooming spending in ways that remote work simply doesn't. Regular haircuts move from every 8-10 weeks to every 4-6 weeks. Daily makeup use (for those who wear it) costs an average of $2,000-$3,000 per year, according to a 2025 Groupon survey. Dry cleaning, laundry service, and the general upkeep of an "office-ready" appearance add another $500-$1,500 annually.

A reasonable estimate for the incremental wardrobe and grooming cost of office attendance is $150-$300 per month, or $1,800-$3,600 per year. For workers in industries with stricter dress codes -- finance, law, consulting -- the figure is higher.

Food and Meal Costs

This is the category where the daily cost of going to the office becomes most viscerally real. Every hybrid worker knows the mental calculation: "I could eat the leftovers in my fridge for free, or I could spend $16 on a mediocre salad from the place near the office." Multiply that daily choice by 250 working days, and the numbers become staggering.

Lunch: the $3,000+ annual swing

The average cost of buying lunch near a U.S. office in 2026 is $15-$18, according to data from Square and Toast. Even the "affordable" options -- fast casual chains, food trucks, pre-made grocery store meals -- typically run $10-$13. Meanwhile, the average cost of making lunch at home is $3-$5, based on USDA food cost data.

The math is stark:

Buying lunch daily (5 days/week): $16 x 250 days = $4,000/year
Making lunch at home: $4 x 250 days = $1,000/year
Annual difference: $3,000

Even hybrid workers who bring lunch most days tend to buy it 1-2 times per week -- a seemingly minor habit that still costs an extra $600-$1,200 per year compared to full remote work.

Coffee: the $1,000 habit

Purchasing a daily coffee at a cafe or office-adjacent coffee shop costs $5-$7 per drink. Making coffee at home costs roughly $0.50-$1.00. For a 5-day office worker buying coffee daily, that's an incremental cost of approximately $1,000-$1,500 per year.

Dinner and convenience foods

An often-missed cost: when you commute, you're more likely to pick up takeout on the way home because you're tired and it's late. A 2024 USDA study found that households with longer commutes spent 22% more on restaurant and takeout food than those with short or no commutes. For the average American household spending $3,639 per year on food away from home, that 22% premium adds roughly $800 per year in extra dinner and convenience spending driven by commuting fatigue.

Childcare and Pet Care Costs

For parents and pet owners, the shift from remote to in-office work triggers some of the largest cost increases of all -- costs that are impossible to reduce through frugality or habit changes.

Before- and after-school care

Remote parents can often handle school drop-off and pickup without additional childcare. But when you're commuting, the math changes. Before-school care programs average $200-$400 per month per child, and after-school programs run $300-$600 per month per child. For a family with two school-age children, the incremental childcare cost of full-time office attendance can reach $12,000-$24,000 per year.

Even for hybrid workers going in 3 days per week, the savings are limited because most programs charge flat monthly rates rather than per-day fees. You might get a partial discount, but you'll likely still pay 60-80% of the full-time rate for a 3-day schedule.

Infant and toddler care

For parents of children under 5, full-time daycare is the dominant cost. The national average for center-based infant care is $1,230 per month ($14,760 per year), according to Care.com's 2025 Cost of Care Survey. In high-cost metro areas, this figure exceeds $2,000 per month. While many working parents need childcare regardless of location, remote work often allows for more flexible arrangements -- a nanny-share 3 days a week, a part-time preschool, or staggered coverage with a partner -- that full-time office mandates make impossible.

Pet care

Pet ownership has surged since 2020, and many of those pets have never known a household where someone wasn't home during the day. When RTO mandates kick in, dog owners in particular face a new cost: dog walking ($15-$25 per walk) or doggy daycare ($25-$50 per day). For a 5-day office worker using daycare, that's $6,000-$12,000 per year. Even a midday dog walker 5 days a week adds $4,500-$6,000 annually.

The Hidden Time Costs

Every cost we've discussed so far is measured in dollars. But the single largest hidden cost of going to the office isn't financial -- it's temporal. Commute time is unpaid labor, and when you put a dollar value on it, the numbers dwarf everything else.

The average commute costs 9 full days per year

The average one-way commute in the United States is 27.6 minutes, according to the U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey. Round trip, that's 55 minutes per day. Over 250 working days, that totals 229 hours per year -- the equivalent of nearly 9.5 full 24-hour days or almost 6 full work weeks spent doing nothing but getting to and from the office.

For workers in the top 25% of commute lengths (45+ minutes each way), annual commute time exceeds 375 hours -- almost 10 full 40-hour work weeks.

What's your time actually worth?

If you earn $75,000 per year (roughly $36 per hour), your 229 annual commuting hours represent $8,244 in lost time value. At $100,000 per year ($48/hour), it's $10,992. At $150,000 per year ($72/hour), it's $16,488.

These aren't theoretical numbers. They represent real time you could spend working (and earning), exercising, sleeping, or being with your family. A 2024 study published in the Journal of Urban Economics found that workers who eliminated their commute reported gaining an average of 40 minutes of additional sleep per night and 30 minutes of additional exercise per week.

The "getting ready" tax

Beyond the commute itself, office days require preparation time that remote days don't. Showering, doing hair and makeup, ironing clothes, packing a bag, making lunch -- these tasks add an estimated 30-60 minutes per office day compared to rolling out of bed and logging in. Over a year of 5-day office attendance, that's another 125-250 hours of lost personal time.

Strategies to Reduce Your RTO Costs

You may not be able to eliminate your RTO costs entirely, but strategic choices can significantly reduce them. Here are proven approaches, ordered by potential savings.

1. Maximize your remote days

This is the highest-leverage strategy. Every day you work from home saves you the full daily cost of commuting, meals, and incidentals. If your company allows any hybrid flexibility, use it fully. Check the Best Days to Go Into the Office tool to identify which days maximize your in-person collaboration while minimizing your total office days.

2. Carpool or use employer transit benefits

Employer-sponsored commuter benefits allow you to set aside up to $325 per month (2026 limit) in pre-tax dollars for transit and parking. If your company offers this, you're effectively getting a 25-35% discount on commuting costs depending on your tax bracket. That's $975-$1,365 in annual tax savings for maxing out the benefit.

Carpooling cuts fuel and parking costs by 50-75% depending on the number of riders. Some metro areas also offer HOV lane access, which can reduce commute time and the associated time-value cost.

3. Meal prep and bring lunch

Bringing lunch from home instead of buying it saves approximately $12 per day, or $3,000 per year for a 5-day office worker. Batch-cooking on Sundays and preparing 3-5 days of lunches takes about 90 minutes -- a time investment that pays roughly $100 per hour in savings.

Similarly, investing in a quality insulated travel mug and making coffee at home eliminates $4-$6 in daily coffee shop spending.

4. Negotiate a commuter stipend

A growing number of companies are offering commuter stipends to offset the costs they're imposing through RTO mandates. According to a 2025 SHRM survey, 27% of employers now offer some form of commuter benefit beyond the standard pre-tax transit program. If your company doesn't offer one, be the employee who asks. Present your calculated costs and make a specific request.

5. Optimize your wardrobe strategy

Build a small, high-quality capsule wardrobe of interchangeable office pieces rather than maintaining a large wardrobe of cheaper items. Five to seven versatile outfits that can be mixed and matched will cover a full work week while reducing both clothing costs and dry cleaning bills. Look for wrinkle-resistant, machine-washable fabrics to eliminate dry cleaning entirely.

6. Consolidate your office days

If you have a hybrid schedule, grouping your office days consecutively (e.g., Tuesday-Wednesday-Thursday) rather than spreading them across the week can reduce costs in subtle ways. You can buy weekly parking passes instead of daily ones. You can meal prep specifically for those three days. And psychologically, having a clear "office block" and "remote block" reduces the daily decision fatigue of preparing for each day differently.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does the average person spend commuting to work per year?
The average American worker with a 25-mile one-way commute spends approximately $8,200-$12,000 per year on commuting costs including gas, vehicle wear, and parking. Public transit commuters spend $1,200-$3,600 per year depending on the city. These figures don't include the time value of commuting, which adds another $8,000-$16,000 annually depending on salary.
What is the total cost of going back to the office full-time?
When you account for commuting, meals, wardrobe, grooming, and the time value of your commute, the average full-time office worker spends $22,000-$48,000 per year in hidden RTO costs. Workers with childcare or pet care responsibilities can face total costs of $32,000-$84,000. Use our RTO Cost Calculator to determine your specific number based on your individual circumstances.
How much do you save by working from home instead of the office?
Each remote workday saves the average worker $40-$80 in direct costs (commuting, meals, incidentals) plus another $30-$65 in time value. Over a year, switching from 5 days in-office to 3 days saves roughly $8,800-$19,000 in direct costs alone. A fully remote worker saves the full $22,000-$48,000 compared to a fully in-office worker.
Should I negotiate for higher pay to offset my RTO costs?
Yes, many compensation experts recommend factoring RTO costs into salary negotiations. Calculate your total annual RTO cost using a tool like our RTO Cost Calculator, then use that data to request either a commuter stipend, meal allowance, or salary adjustment. According to SHRM data, 27% of employers now offer some form of commuter benefit, and presenting a specific, calculated cost makes your case much stronger than a vague request.
How do I calculate the time value of my commute?
Divide your annual salary by 2,080 (the number of working hours in a year) to get your hourly rate. Then multiply that rate by your daily round-trip commute time in hours, then by your number of commuting days per year. For example, a $100,000 salary with a 55-minute daily round-trip commute over 250 days equals $48/hour x 0.917 hours x 250 days = $11,004 per year in time value.
What are the biggest hidden costs of returning to the office?
The most commonly overlooked RTO costs include: the time value of commuting (often the largest single cost at $8,000-$16,000/year), increased takeout and dinner spending due to commute fatigue ($800+/year), vehicle depreciation and maintenance ($1,500-$3,000/year), childcare schedule changes ($6,000-$24,000/year for parents), and pet care ($4,500-$12,000/year for dog owners). Most workers only think about gas and parking when estimating their commute costs.
